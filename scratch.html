About the Maker

Forged, not
manufactured.

Every bracelet I make starts as raw metal wire and ends as a wearable piece of art. I use a micro welding torch and precision hand tools to fuse each link individually — no molds, no casting, no shortcuts.

My background in precious metals processing and placer gold prospecting gives me a deep, material-level respect for silver, copper, and gold. That knowledge shapes everything from how I source wire to how I finish a clasp.

How It's Made

The Process

  1. 01 Material Selection

    Every piece starts with the right wire. Gauge and alloy determine the final look and feel.

    • Choose wire gauge: 18g for bold links, 22g for delicate chains
    • Select metal: sterling silver (.925), fine silver (.999), or copper
    • Assess purity and spot value before cutting
  2. 02 Forming the Links

    Wire is coiled around a steel mandrel, then cut into individual jump rings.

    • Wind wire tightly on a mandrel to set the inner diameter
    • Cut rings with flush-cut jeweler's shears
    • Shape each ring to a perfect round by hand with pliers
  3. 03 Welding & Joining

    This is the step most makers skip — true torch welding, not just glue or cold-closure.

    • Apply borax flux to prevent oxidation at the seam
    • Use micro torch to flow solder or fuse the metal directly
    • Quench in water, then pickle to remove flux residue
  4. 04 Finishing

    The last step determines the surface character — bright polish, brushed, or patina.

    • Tumble in steel shot for an even high polish
    • Hand-buff with progressively finer compounds
    • Optional: apply liver of sulfur for oxidized depth
